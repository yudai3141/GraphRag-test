# Symptoms as a Property of Memory Structure: A Fear-Structure Graph for Simulated PTSD Patients in Therapist Training

*CHI full-paper draft (first full pass). Study 1 is written from current results; Study 2 is under design and stated as a plan, with specifics left blank on purpose.*

## Abstract

Training clinicians to deliver trauma-focused therapy for post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) requires practice patients, but real patients cannot be used for rehearsal and actor-based standardized patients are costly and do not expose the patient's internal state. Large-language-model (LLM) simulated patients are increasingly used for this purpose, but they typically condition symptoms on a static prompt. We identify three consequences of this design for training: the associative process behind an utterance is not inspectable, turn-to-turn consistency is not mechanistically enforced, and the patient's state cannot be modified by treatment, so exposure and extinction cannot be practiced. We take a different design principle, drawn directly from Foa and Kozak's theory of fear [Foa1986]: because that theory holds that fear is a *structure* in memory and that its pathology and its treatment are properties of that structure, a simulated patient's symptoms should be *produced by* a memory structure rather than specified by a prompt. We operationalize this principle as a dialogue agent whose reactivity is generated by spreading activation over a two-layer fear-structure graph, and we map the CAPS-5 intrusion criteria onto measurements over the graph. Study 1 validates the graph. Using 80 utterances stratified by trauma-relatedness, we find that (i) a single spreading process, read at different node types, yields separable measures for physiological reactivity (B5), psychological distress (B4), and intrusive memory (B1); (ii) these measures depend on graph propagation rather than on the entry-step text match; and (iii) the graph over-generalizes to trauma-unrelated cues relative to a size-matched healthy graph built by the same pipeline. Study 2 [under design] evaluates the agent with clinicians in the loop. We discuss design implications of an inspectable and modifiable patient model for clinical-training systems.

## 1. Introduction

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is characterized by re-experiencing symptoms that persist after a traumatic event, including intrusive memories, flashbacks, and heightened reactivity to cues that resemble the trauma. Training clinicians to treat PTSD — for example with exposure therapy — requires supervised practice, but practicing on real patients is not appropriate, and actor-based standardized patients are expensive, inconsistent across sessions, and unable to reveal the patient's internal state [PATIENT-Ψ]. Simulated patients — conversational agents that a trainee can interview — have therefore been proposed as a scalable alternative.

Most current LLM-based simulated patients represent the patient as a static textual description and generate behavior by role-play. We identify three properties of this design that limit its use as a training tool. First, the internal associative process that produces an utterance is not inspectable, so a supervisor cannot ground feedback in *why* the patient reacted. Second, consistency across turns depends on the model maintaining a persona rather than on an underlying mechanism, and can drift without any detectable cause. Third, the patient's representation is fixed at prompt time and is not altered by the interaction, so the mechanism that exposure therapy targets — repeated safe activation of the fear response leading to extinction — has nothing to act on.

We attribute these limitations to a single design choice: whether symptoms are specified extrinsically (conditioned on a prompt) or produced intrinsically (by a memory mechanism). The second option is not merely an engineering preference; it is the position of the clinical theory we adopt. Foa and Kozak [Foa1986], building on Lang's bio-informational theory [Lang1979], argue that fear is not a single concept but a *structure* in memory — an associative network of stimulus, response, and meaning elements — and that both its pathology (erroneous, unusually strong associations) and its treatment (emotional processing, i.e., modification of the structure through corrective information) are properties of that structure rather than of a symptom list. We take this theoretical claim as a design principle: in a simulated PTSD patient, symptoms should be produced by the organization of a memory structure, so that they are inspectable and modifiable by the same operations the theory ascribes to therapy.

This paper reports a computational realization of that principle and a first validation of it. We make four contributions. (1) A memory design that translates Foa and Kozak's fear structure into a two-layer graph, separating an associative fear-structure layer from an episodic-memory layer (§3.1). (2) An operationalization of the CAPS-5 intrusion criteria as measurements over the graph, with the observation that one spreading-activation process, read at different node types, yields separable symptom measures (§3.3). (3) Study 1, a quantitative evaluation showing that the graph's reactivity descends with trauma-relatedness, is produced by propagation rather than by surface text similarity, and over-generalizes relative to a size-matched healthy control built by the same pipeline (§4). (4) Study 2 [under design], a clinician-facing evaluation of the resulting agent (§5). We frame the work as a step toward simulated patients whose internal state is both observable and, in principle, modifiable by treatment, and discuss the resulting design implications (§6).

## 2. Related Work

**Simulated and standardized patients for clinical training.** Standardized patients — actors trained to portray a case — are established in clinical education but are costly, vary between sessions, and cannot expose their internal state to the learner. Virtual and screen-based patients add reproducibility and lower cost, and recent systems use LLMs to make simulated patients conversational; PATIENT-Ψ [PATIENT-Ψ] constructs patients from cognitive-behavioral case-conceptualization diagrams and improves clinician-rated skill relative to textbook study and role-play. This line of work establishes that conversational simulated patients support training. Our contribution is orthogonal: rather than conditioning behavior on a description, we generate it from a running memory mechanism, which makes the cause of a reaction inspectable and the patient state modifiable. *[TODO: add 2–4 references on virtual patients, medical conversational agents, and explainable agents in CHI/CSCW.]*

**Computational models of PTSD and memory.** Several cognitive theories model intrusive re-experiencing: Foa and Kozak's fear structure [Foa1986]; the dual-representation account of flashbacks as an imbalance between a strong sensory and a weak contextual representation [Brewin2010]; and Ehlers and Clark's cognitive model of PTSD maintenance [EhlersClark2000]. Computationally, ACT-R and adaptive-network models reproduce aspects of PTSD symptomatology [Smith2021]. These models fit symptom phenomena but do not provide a running, inspectable trauma-memory mechanism built directly on a Foa-faithful fear structure, which is the gap we address.

**Spreading activation and memory in interactive agents.** Our recall mechanism derives from the ACT-R tradition and from Collins and Loftus's spreading-activation theory of semantic memory [Collins1975], in which a cue activates a node and activation propagates to associated nodes with decreasing strength. Dialogue agents have used related mechanisms to model human-like remembering and forgetting. We use spreading activation not as a retrieval method but as the substrate from which clinical symptom measures are read. *[TODO: cite and connect to our prior work on human-like remembering and forgetting in dialogue agents.]*

## 3. System: A Fear-Structure Graph Agent

### 3.1 Design: from Foa's elements to a two-layer graph

Foa and Kozak [Foa1986], building on Lang [Lang1979], model fear as an associative structure of three element types: *stimulus* (perceptual and situational features), *response* (bodily and behavioral reactions), and *meaning* (the interpretation of the stimulus and response, e.g., "I am in danger"). Two properties are central to our design: activating part of the structure activates the whole, so a partial cue can trigger the network; and pathology arises from erroneous and unusually strong associations rather than from any distinct node. Lang [Lang1979] additionally locates emotion in the response elements.

We map these element types to node types and their associations to edge types, and add an episodic layer for autobiographical memory. The **fear-structure layer** contains stimulus, response, and meaning nodes; meaning is kept as open vocabulary rather than a small fixed category set. Its edges are EVOKES (stimulus–response), MEANS (stimulus or response–meaning), CO_OCCURS (stimuli co-present in a scene), and SIMILAR (semantically related stimuli, modeling generalization). The **episodic layer** contains autobiographical event nodes linked to the fear structure by RECALLS (stimulus→episode) and BINDS (episode↔its response and meaning). BINDS encodes a memory as a bundle of the stimulus, response, and meaning present at encoding, following the dual-representation view that recalling an episode re-activates its associated reactions and interpretations [Brewin2010]. The graph is built from a first-person narrative by LLM extraction; each episode is assigned a valence label (negative, neutral, positive) at extraction time from the narrated event, independently of the cues used later to probe the graph.

To keep the model faithful to the theory rather than to convenience, we removed several constructs present in an earlier version that the theory does not license: a fixed set of "core-evaluation" categories, a single danger hub, an externally imposed threat bias, per-relation edge weights, and negative-to-negative shortcut edges. Associations are treated as undirected and symmetric with essentially uniform weights, and any bias toward threat is left to emerge from the structure (the relative number of negative episodes and meanings) rather than being injected; we note the absence of associative-strength and directional asymmetry as a modeling limitation. Only SIMILAR carries a weight, reflecting semantic proximity. Figure A shows the schema and Figure C a graph built from a first-person account.

### 3.2 Spreading activation

Recall proceeds by spreading activation. The input utterance is embedded and activation is seeded at the nearest stimulus nodes — the *entry step* (top three stimuli with cosine similarity ≥ 0.20). Activation then propagates along edges: it decays by a fixed factor per hop (0.6) and is divided across a node's out-edges (fan effect); because associations are undirected it also propagates backward, and propagation halts after three hops. The activation accumulated at each node is then read out, with fear read from the response nodes following Lang [Lang1979]. Figure D depicts the entry–spread–readout flow.

### 3.3 Mapping the CAPS-5 intrusion criteria to graph measurements

The CAPS-5 is the standard structured interview for PTSD and scores several symptom clusters; we operationalize the intrusion (B) cluster because it is the core re-experiencing symptomatology and the most direct target for a memory-based model. The central property is that the *same* spreading activation, read at *different* node types, corresponds to different intrusion criteria, which is possible because stimulus, response, meaning, and episode are distinct node types. We read physiological reactivity (B5) as R, the summed activation of response nodes; psychological distress (B4) as M, the summed activation of threat-meaning nodes; and intrusive memory (B1) as E, the summed activation of negative-episode nodes. Flashback (B3) is a design target rather than a measured quantity here: following dual-representation theory [Brewin2010] it corresponds to the ratio S/(S+C) between an episode's sensory and contextual sub-representations, introduced in §6. Nightmares (B2) require a sleep/dream mechanism outside our scope. Figure B illustrates one activation wave read three ways.

### 3.4 Interactive front-end

The agent is designed to be interviewed while its internal associations are observed. A dialogue front-end displays, beneath each response, the chain that produced it (entry stimulus, responses, meanings, recalled episodes), and a live heat-map of the graph shows the distribution of activation as the conversation proceeds, making the cause of a reaction available to a supervising clinician. *[TODO: the current front-end predates the theory-faithful ontology and has not been migrated to it; we will either migrate it before Study 2 or report it through captured examples, noted in Limitations.]*

## 4. Study 1 — Validating the memory graph

Before adding dialogue and clinical scoring, we test whether the memory graph behaves as a fear structure should: reacting more strongly to trauma-related cues than to unrelated ones. This validation is a precondition for anything built on the graph.

### 4.1 Method

We built three graphs with an identical extraction pipeline. The **PTSD (trauma-only)** graph is derived from a first-person PTSD narrative; the **PTSD (balanced)** graph adds ordinary and positive daily-life material to the same narrative, so that one person's graph contains both trauma and non-trauma memories; and the **healthy control** is derived from an independent, non-trauma daily-life narrative matched in length. The three are comparable in size (approximately 470–570 nodes and 1000–1300 edges) to avoid confounding comparisons with scale. A benign narrative yields only a small fear structure — the extractor finds little fear material in ordinary life, itself a result — so we matched narrative length to keep node counts comparable.

We probe each graph with a battery of 80 short utterances of the kind a partner or friend might say, stratified into four levels by trauma-relatedness (20 per level): L1 directly evokes the trauma, L2 is trauma-related, L3 is everyday-ambiguous, and L4 is neutral and trauma-unrelated. The levels are an independent axis (LLM-generated, intended for clinical review) rather than embedding distance, to avoid circularity with the entry step. For each cue we run spreading activation and compute R, M, and E, reporting the per-level mean over 20 cues. A curve descending from L1 to L4 indicates that the graph separates trauma-related from neutral cues; a shallow descent — high reactivity persisting at the related L2 and ambiguous L3 — is the signature of over-generalization.

Because R, M, and E grow with graph size and are not comparable across graphs, we additionally define a scale-free *fear-reaction rate*: a cue counts as eliciting a fear reaction when both the cue strongly matches memory (cosine similarity to the nearest stimulus at the entry step ≥ 0.35) and its recalled episodes are negative-dominant. The rate is the percentage of a level's 20 cues meeting both conditions. To test whether reactivity depends on the mechanism, we compare the full model against two ablations: *no_spread*, which seeds the entry step but disables propagation, and *no_bind*, which removes only the BINDS edges.

### 4.2 Results

**Experiment 1 (Figure 1): the phenomenon.** On the PTSD (trauma-only) graph, all three measures descend from L1 to L4 (L1/L4 ratios: R 1.54, M 1.40, E 1.65), indicating that the graph separates fear-relevant from neutral cues. The descent is shallow: reactivity at the related L2 equals that at L1 (E: 0.101 vs. 0.100), remains high at the ambiguous L3 (E 0.084), and falls only at the neutral L4 (E 0.061). The three curves differ in both level and slope, indicating that the three symptom measures are separable rather than one measure relabeled.

**Experiment 2 (Figure 2): mechanism and dissociation.** With propagation disabled (*no_spread*), R and E are zero; this confirms, if near-definitionally, that reactivity is produced by propagation and not by the entry match alone. Removing only the BINDS edges (*no_bind*) reduces intrusive-memory recall E by 56% (0.100→0.044) while leaving bodily response R essentially unchanged (0.198→0.187). The informative result is not that removing edges reduces activation but that the reduction is *selective*: the two layers perform distinct functions, and BINDS is specific to episodic recall. *[TODO: recast from "the mechanism is necessary" to a dissociation result, and add an edge-count-matched random-removal control to establish that the E reduction is specific to BINDS rather than to deleting a comparable number of edges.]*

**Experiment 3 (Figure 3): specificity.** Using the scale-free fear-reaction rate, the PTSD (trauma-only) graph reacts to 75–85% of L1–L3 cues, including 75% of the ambiguous L3 cues, and falls to 30% only at the neutral L4. The size-matched healthy graph reacts to 25–30% of L1–L3 cues and to 5% at L4, responding weakly to threat-themed cues without over-generalizing to neutral ones. The balanced graph, adding the same person's ordinary memories, lies between the two and shifts the curve down (85%→40% at L1). This indicates that a trauma-only corpus inflates apparent reactivity because it contains only threat material, so a balanced narrative and a healthy control are necessary, not optional, to substantiate a claim of over-generalization.

### 4.3 Summary

Read at different node types, one spreading process yields separable CAPS-5 intrusion measures; these are produced by propagation and are in part carried by a dedicated episodic-binding layer; and the graph over-generalizes to trauma-unrelated cues in a way a healthy graph built by the same pipeline does not. The memory structure thus satisfies the basic requirements for the internal mechanism of a simulated PTSD patient.

## 5. Study 2 — Clinician-facing evaluation [under design]

Study 1 validates the memory structure in isolation but does not establish that the resulting agent is a credible and useful simulated patient for a clinician. Study 2 addresses this with clinicians in the loop; its design is being finalized and is stated here as a plan, with specifics deliberately left blank.

We are considering three forms. (A) *CAPS-5 scoring*: construct a set of simulated-patient personas, elicit responses within the CAPS-5 framework, and test whether the resulting symptom profiles and diagnostic thresholds match clinical expectation, with a clinical psychologist auditing the scoring framework and sampled cases rather than rating every case, because expert time does not scale to large samples. (B) *Clinician interaction study*: trainee or expert clinicians interview the agent and rate its fidelity as a PTSD patient and the utility of its internal-association visualization for training, relative to a prompt-conditioned baseline. (C) *Therapeutic dynamic*: safe, exposure-like dialogue is used to weaken pathological edges, testing whether the fear-reaction rate declines over repeated sessions, which would evaluate the agent's distinguishing property — a patient state modifiable by treatment.

## 6. Discussion

**Inspectable symptoms.** Because reactivity is produced by the structure rather than specified by a prompt, each reaction is accompanied by its generating chain: the entry stimulus, the responses and meanings it activated, and the episodes it recalled. For a training tool this supports grounding supervisory feedback in the cause of a reaction, and the visualization of §3.4 is the interface to that explanation.

**A modifiable patient state.** Modeling exposure therapy as the weakening of pathological edges, rather than the deletion of memories, makes recovery an operation on the same structure that produces symptoms. This affords an interaction unavailable to prompt-conditioned patients — conducting exposure across sessions and observing the fear response decline — which Study 2's therapeutic-dynamic option would evaluate.

**Flashback as a design target.** We treat flashback (B3) via dual-representation theory [Brewin2010], under which a flashback is a dissociation between a strongly activated sensory representation and a weak contextual one, producing a sense of present-time recurrence. On the graph this is the imbalance S/(S+C) once each episode is split into sensory and contextual sub-nodes. The model is intended to cover the onset of this imbalance and to leave control-level dissociation, which a purely associative network cannot express, out of scope.

**A methodological point.** Study 1 shows that a trauma-only narrative confounds a model's over-generalization with the fact that the narrative contains only threat material. A balanced narrative and a size-matched healthy control separate the two, and we recommend both as defaults for structure-based patient models.

## 7. Conclusion

Reading a single spreading-activation process at different node types of a theory-faithful fear-structure graph yields separable measures corresponding to CAPS-5 intrusion criteria, and these measures over-generalize to trauma-unrelated cues relative to a size-matched healthy control. This provides an observable and, in principle, modifiable basis for a simulated PTSD patient, consistent with Foa and Kozak's account of fear as a structure whose pathology and treatment are properties of that structure. Study 2 [under design] will evaluate this basis with clinicians in the loop.
